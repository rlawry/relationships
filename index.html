How to Read These Graphs

Every graph shows a relationship between two variables. Your job is to identify what type of relationship it is — or predict which direction y moves as x increases. Here is what to look for.

Proportional

The graph passes through the origin (0, 0) — meaning when x is zero, y is also zero. The relationship stays tied to zero at all times. The line or curve may be steep or shallow, but it always starts at the corner. Both straight lines through the origin and curves through the origin count as proportional.

Linear (nonproportional)

The graph is a perfectly straight line, but it does NOT pass through the origin. When x is zero, y already has a value — there is a built-in starting amount. The line rises or falls at a steady, constant rate. Look for: straight line, does not cross at (0, 0).

Negative linear / opposite

The graph is a straight line that slopes downward from left to right. As x increases, y decreases. The variables move in opposite directions at a steady rate. The line may or may not cross the origin — what matters is that it is straight and falling.

Nonlinear

The graph is a curve — the rate of change is not constant. It bends. Some nonlinear graphs start above zero on the y-axis and curve upward. Others may start near zero but still bend. The key is that the shape is not a straight line. Do not mistake a gentle curve for a straight line — look carefully at whether the steepness changes as you move along the graph.

Direction question: Increases or Decreases?

When asked "as x increases, y...?", trace the graph from left to right. If the graph goes up as you move right, y increases. If it goes down, y decreases. This applies to all graph types. If the axes are flipped, focus on the labeled directions — do not rely on which way the line visually points.

When the axes are flipped (Regents Mode)

In Normal and Hardcore Regents modes, one or both axes may be reversed. The numbers still tell you the true direction. Look at the axis labels and tick values — if the axis counts downward, the graph is flipped. The relationship type does not change just because an axis is reversed. A straight line is still straight. A curve is still a curve. Focus on shape, not orientation.
